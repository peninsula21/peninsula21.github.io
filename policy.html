Section A --- Health & Safety

PurposeTo ensure the health and safety of all students,
ScopeAll students, staff, contractors, volunteers and

Legal Basis Occupational Health and Safety Act 2004 (Vic);

Education and Training Reform Act 2006 (Vic); VRQA

Minimum Standards.

Review Cycle Annual

1. Policy Statement

Consensus 21 Education Ltd (Peninsula 21) is committed to providing a safe environment for all members of the school community. The school will maintain adequate first aid resources, trained personnel, and clear procedures to respond to illness and injury.

2. First Aid Officers

The Principal must ensure that at all times during school hours and school activities, at least one qualified First Aid Officer is present on campus or accompanying students off-campus.

At least one staff member per 50 students holds a current first aid

qualification (HLTAID012 or equivalent).

First Aid Officers are clearly identified to students, staff and

visitors.

First aid qualifications must be renewed every three years.

3. First Aid Facilities

A dedicated first aid room is maintained, accessible to all students and

staff.

First aid kits are maintained and checked termly. Contents comply with

relevant standards.

An automated external defibrillator (AED) is maintained on campus with

staff trained in its use.

A register of all first aid incidents is maintained.

4. Responding to Illness or Injury

Assess the situation and ensure safety of the area.

Call for a qualified First Aid Officer immediately.

Administer first aid appropriate to the situation.

Contact parents/guardians for all significant incidents.

Call 000 for any life-threatening emergency.

Complete a First Aid Incident Record on the same day.

Notify the Principal as soon as practicable.

5. Student Medical Information

The school collects and maintains up-to-date medical information for all enrolled students, including allergies, chronic conditions, and medications. This information is stored securely and accessible to relevant staff.

6. Medication Administration

Prescription medications may only be administered with written authority

from a parent/guardian and, where required, a medical practitioner.

Medications are stored securely and a record of administration is

maintained.

Staff do not administer non-prescribed medications except in emergency

situations.

7. Communication

Parents/guardians are notified promptly of any first aid treatment administered to their child. All serious incidents are reported to the Principal and documented in the incident register.

A2 Section A — Health & Safety

Anaphylaxis Management Policy

PurposeTo minimise the risk of anaphylaxis and to ensure

Scope All students, staff and visitors at Peninsula 21.

Legal Basis Ministerial Order No. 706 --- Anaphylaxis

Management in Schools; Education and Training

Reform Act 2006 (Vic).

Review Cycle Annual

1. Policy Statement

Consensus 21 Education Ltd is committed to providing a safe environment for students at risk of anaphylaxis. This policy complies with Ministerial Order No. 706 and is based on the Anaphylaxis Guidelines published by the Department of Education.

2. Risk Minimisation

The school maintains a register of all students identified as being at

risk of anaphylaxis.

Individual Anaphylaxis Management Plans (IAMPs) are developed for each

at-risk student in consultation with parents and the student\'s medical

practitioner.

School activities, menus and environments are reviewed to minimise

allergen exposure.

Students are not permitted to share food.

Communication to the school community includes reminders about allergen

risks.

3. Individual Anaphylaxis Management Plans

An IAMP must be prepared for each student at risk and must include:

Information about the student\'s medical condition and known allergens.

Strategies to minimise the risk of exposure.

The emergency response to be followed if anaphylaxis occurs.

An ASCIA Action Plan signed by the student\'s medical practitioner.

IAMPs are reviewed annually, when the student\'s condition changes, or

following an anaphylactic reaction.

4. Adrenaline Auto-Injectors (EpiPens)

Students identified as at risk must have a prescribed adrenaline

auto-injector (e.g. EpiPen) at school at all times.

The school maintains a general-use EpiPen for emergency use only, stored

in the first aid room and other accessible locations as determined by

the Principal.

EpiPens are checked regularly and replaced before expiry.

5. Staff Training

All staff who supervise students complete anaphylaxis awareness training

annually.

At least two staff members on campus at all times hold a current

Anaphylaxis Management qualification.

All staff know the location of EpiPens and the emergency response

procedure.

6. Emergency Response

Lay the student flat --- do not allow them to stand.

Administer the student\'s EpiPen (or general-use EpiPen if unavailable)

to the outer mid-thigh.

Call 000 immediately --- state \'anaphylaxis\'.

Contact the student\'s parents/guardians.

Administer a second EpiPen after 5 minutes if no improvement.

Stay with the student until ambulance arrives.

Complete an incident report on the same day.

Students who have received an EpiPen must be transported to hospital by

ambulance even if symptoms improve.

7. Excursions and Off-Campus Activities

IAMPs and relevant EpiPens accompany at-risk students on all excursions. At least one trained staff member accompanies at-risk students at all times.

B1 Section B — Governance

Board Governance Charter

PurposeTo establish the governance framework, roles and

Scope Board of Directors, Sponsor Members, Principal,

Company Secretary.

Legal Basis Corporations Act 2001 (Cth); ACNC Legislation; ETRR

2017; Constitution of Consensus 21 Education Ltd.

Review Cycle Annual

1. Role of the Board

The Board of Directors is responsible for the governance, strategic direction, and financial oversight of Consensus 21 Education Ltd. The Board ensures the Company operates in accordance with its Constitution, objects, and all Relevant Laws.

The Board does not manage the day-to-day operations of the school --- this is the responsibility of the Principal --- but retains oversight of all significant decisions.

2. Board Composition

The Board consists of no fewer than 3 and no more than 9 Directors.

Up to 3 Directors are Sponsor-Nominated Directors appointed by the

Sponsor Members.

Up to 6 Directors are Board Elected Directors appointed by the Board.

The Board shall at all times seek a balance of skills including

education, finance, law, and community engagement.

3. Director Responsibilities

Each Director must:

Act in good faith in the best interests of the Company and its students.

Exercise due care and diligence in their role.

Disclose and manage conflicts of interest.

Maintain confidentiality of Board deliberations.

Attend at least 75% of Board meetings in each year.

Complete annual Fit and Proper Person Declarations as required by the

VRQA.

Uphold the Protected Purpose and the school\'s values at all times.

4. Chair and Deputy Chair

The Chairperson leads the Board, chairs all meetings, and acts as the primary liaison between the Board and the Principal. The Deputy Chairperson acts in the Chair\'s absence. Both are elected annually by the Board.

5. Board Meetings

The Board meets at least 8 times per year, at least once each school

term.

Meetings are convened by the Chair or on the request of any 2 Directors.

At least 5 Business Days\' notice and an agenda must be provided.

A quorum is a majority of Directors then in office.

Each Director has one vote. The Chair has a casting vote in a tied vote.

6. Conflicts of Interest

Directors must declare any actual, potential, or perceived conflict of interest as soon as they become aware of it. A Director with a material personal interest in a matter must not participate in deliberations or vote on that matter. All declarations are recorded in the Conflict of Interest Register.

7. Board-Principal Relationship

The Board appoints and supports the Principal and holds them accountable for school performance. The Principal is responsible for operational management and reports to the Board at each meeting. The Principal attends Board meetings but does not vote.

8. Board Effectiveness

The Board commits to annual self-assessment of its performance and governance practices. The Board will ensure adequate professional development and induction for all Directors.

9. Protected Matters

Certain decisions require both a Special Resolution of Members and a Mission Protection Resolution of Sponsor Members. These include changes to the educational philosophy, Sponsor Member rights, and winding up. The Board must not take any action inconsistent with the Protected Purpose without a Mission Protection Resolution.

B3 Section B — Governance

Conflict of Interest Policy

PurposeTo ensure that actual, potential and perceived

Scope All Directors, the Principal, Company Secretary,

and senior staff.

Legal Basis Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) s.191; ACNC Governance

Standards; Constitution clause 6.7 and 18.3.

Review Cycle Every 2 years

1. Policy Statement

Consensus 21 Education Ltd requires all responsible persons to act in the best interests of the Company. Where a personal interest conflicts --- or may appear to conflict --- with those duties, that interest must be disclosed and managed.

2. What is a Conflict of Interest?

A conflict of interest arises when a person\'s private interests (financial or otherwise) could improperly influence their decisions in their role. This includes:

Financial interests in a supplier, contractor or other entity doing

business with the school.

Personal relationships with employees, students or suppliers.

Outside employment or business activities.

Receipt of gifts, hospitality or benefits from parties doing business

with the school.

3. Disclosure Requirements

All responsible persons must:

Disclose any actual, potential or perceived conflict of interest as soon

as they become aware of it.

Complete an annual Conflict of Interest Declaration.

Notify the Chair (or Principal for staff) immediately if a conflict

arises during the year.

4. Management of Conflicts

Once a conflict is disclosed, the Board (or Principal for staff matters) will determine how to manage it. Options include:

The person recuses themselves from deliberations and decisions on the

matter.

The person is excluded from access to related information.

The person resigns from a position creating the conflict.

For Directors: a Director with a material personal interest must not

vote on the matter and must not be present during deliberations, as

required by the Corporations Act 2001.

5. Conflict of Interest Register

The Company Secretary maintains a Conflict of Interest Register recording all declared interests. The register is reviewed at each Board meeting and available to the VRQA on request.

6. Gifts and Hospitality

Responsible persons must not accept gifts worth more than \$50 from parties doing business with or seeking to do business with the school. All gifts received must be declared and recorded. Gifts must never influence decisions.

7. Breach

Failure to disclose a conflict of interest may constitute Terminable Conduct under the Constitution and may be reported to relevant regulators.

B4 Section B — Governance

Sub-Committee Terms of Reference

PurposeTo establish the scope, authority and operation of

Scope All Board sub-committees and their members.

Legal Basis Constitution of Consensus 21 Education Ltd (clause

6.10).

Review Cycle Annual or upon establishment of a new sub-committee

1. Establishment

The Board may establish sub-committees to assist it in carrying out its functions. Each sub-committee is established by Board resolution setting out its purpose, membership and delegated authority.

2. Standard Provisions --- All Sub-Committees

Each sub-committee must include at least one Director of the Company.

Sub-committee members need not be Directors or Members of the Company.

Sub-committees may only exercise powers expressly delegated by the

Board.

No sub-committee may resolve any Protected Matter.

Sub-committees report to the Board at each Board meeting.

Minutes of sub-committee meetings are provided to the Board.

3. Standing Sub-Committees

### 3.1 Finance and Audit Committee

Purpose: Oversee financial management, risk, and audit functions.

Membership: At least 2 Directors including the Chair, plus the Principal

and Business Manager (ex officio).

Meets: At least quarterly.

Authority: Reviews financial reports, monitors budget compliance,

recommends annual budget to Board, oversees internal and external audit.

### 3.2 Education and Curriculum Committee

Purpose: Advise the Board on educational performance, curriculum, and

the Protected Purpose.

Membership: At least 2 Directors, the Principal, and up to 2 co-opted

members with educational expertise.

Meets: At least termly.

Authority: Reviews educational data and outcomes, advises on curriculum

initiatives, monitors Protected Purpose implementation.

### 3.3 People and Culture Committee

Purpose: Oversee staffing, performance, and culture matters.

Membership: At least 2 Directors and the Principal (except when the

Principal is the subject of discussion).

Meets: As required, at least twice per year.

Authority: Reviews HR policies, oversees Principal performance review,

advises on staff culture and wellbeing.

4. Ad Hoc Sub-Committees

The Board may establish ad hoc sub-committees for specific projects or purposes. Terms of reference, membership and delegated authority are set at the time of establishment and cease upon completion of the task.

C1 Section C — HR & Workplace

Fraud and Corruption Prevention Policy

PurposeTo prevent, detect and respond to fraud, corruption

Scope All Directors, staff, contractors, volunteers and

agents of the Company.

Legal Basis Corporations Act 2001 (Cth); Criminal Code Act 1995

(Cth); Crimes Act 1958 (Vic); ACNC Governance

Standards.

Review Cycle Annual

1. Policy Statement

Consensus 21 Education Ltd has zero tolerance for fraud, corruption, theft or any form of financial misconduct. The Company is committed to protecting its assets, public funding and reputation through strong internal controls and a culture of integrity and accountability.

2. What is Fraud?

Fraud includes any intentional act or omission designed to deceive, resulting in the Company or another party suffering loss, including:

Theft of money, property or assets belonging to the Company.

Misappropriation or misuse of Company funds.

Falsification of records, accounts, expense claims or timesheets.

Accepting or offering bribes, kickbacks or secret commissions.

Conflicts of interest not disclosed and managed.

Improper use of the Company\'s name, property or resources.

3. Prevention

The Board maintains a Delegations Register with clear financial

authorities and dual-signatory requirements.

All financial transactions are properly authorised and documented.

Accounts are audited annually by an independent registered auditor.

Staff are trained in financial controls and their obligations on

induction.

No single person has unchecked control over financial processes.

4. Detection and Reporting

All persons are encouraged and expected to report suspected fraud. Reports may be made to:

The Principal (for staff matters).

The Board Chair (for matters involving the Principal or a Director).

Anonymously via the Whistleblower process (Policy C3).

Reports will be taken seriously and investigated promptly. Victimisation

of a person who reports in good faith is a serious disciplinary matter.

5. Investigation

The Board Chair (or a Director appointed by the Board) will oversee investigation of fraud allegations. Where the allegation involves a Director, an independent external investigator may be appointed. Investigations will be conducted confidentially and in accordance with natural justice.

6. Consequences

Substantiated fraud may result in dismissal, recovery of losses, referral to police and/or regulatory bodies, and civil action. There are no exceptions based on seniority or length of service.

C2 Section C — HR & Workplace

Privacy Policy

PurposeTo ensure the Company handles personal information

Scope All staff, Directors, contractors and volunteers

who handle personal information.

Legal Basis Privacy and Data Protection Act 2014 (Vic); Privacy

Act 1988 (Cth); Health Records Act 2001 (Vic).

Review Cycle Annual

1. Policy Statement

Consensus 21 Education Ltd collects, uses, stores and discloses personal information about students, families and staff in order to provide educational services. The Company is committed to protecting privacy and handling personal information in accordance with applicable law.

2. What Personal Information We Collect

Students: name, date of birth, address, enrolment details, academic

records, health and medical information, photos and video.

Families: contact details, financial information, communications.

Staff: employment records, qualifications, payroll, health information,

background check results.

3. How We Collect It

We collect personal information directly from individuals where practicable --- through enrolment forms, employment applications, medical forms, and direct contact. We may also collect information from third parties such as previous schools, medical practitioners, or government agencies where permitted.

4. How We Use It

To provide education and support services to students.

To communicate with families about their child\'s progress and school

matters.

To comply with legislative and regulatory requirements.

To manage employment relationships.

For school operations including billing, records management, and safety.

5. Disclosure

We do not disclose personal information to third parties except:

Where required or permitted by law (e.g. mandatory reporting, VRQA,

ACNC).

With the consent of the individual (or parent/guardian for students).

To service providers bound by confidentiality obligations.

6. Storage and Security

Personal information is stored securely --- whether in physical or electronic form --- with access limited to authorised staff. We take reasonable steps to protect information from misuse, interference, loss, and unauthorised access.

7. Access and Correction

Individuals have the right to access their personal information held by the school and to request corrections. Requests should be made in writing to the Principal. We will respond within 30 days.

8. Data Breaches

In the event of a data breach, the Company will act promptly to contain it, assess the risk, and notify affected individuals and relevant authorities where required by law.

C3 Section C — HR & Workplace

Whistleblower Policy

PurposeTo encourage reporting of misconduct and to protect

Scope All Directors, staff, contractors, suppliers,

volunteers, and associates.

Legal Basis Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) Part 9.4AAA (as amended

by Treasury Laws Amendment (Enhancing Whistleblower

Protections) Act 2019).

Review Cycle Annual

1. Policy Statement

Consensus 21 Education Ltd is committed to maintaining a culture of honesty, transparency and accountability. The Company encourages anyone who has reasonable grounds to suspect misconduct to report it, and guarantees protection from victimisation for those who do.

2. Who Can Make a Report?

Any current or former Director, officer, employee, contractor, supplier, associate, or relative of such a person may make a whistleblower report.

3. What Can Be Reported?

Reports may relate to:

Fraud, theft, misappropriation or financial misconduct.

Breach of the Corporations Act or ACNC legislation.

Misconduct by a Director or staff member.

Conduct that represents a danger to the public, the environment, or the

health or safety of any person.

A cover-up of any of the above.

4. How to Make a Report

Reports can be made to:

The Board Chair --- directly or by email marked \'Confidential ---

Whistleblower\'.

Any Director other than the person the report concerns.

Anonymously by sealed letter addressed to the Board Chair.

Reports may also be made to external bodies including ASIC, the ATO, or

a legal practitioner.

5. Protections

The Corporations Act provides the following protections to eligible whistleblowers:

Confidentiality --- the identity of the discloser will not be revealed

without consent except where required by law.

Protection from victimisation --- it is illegal to dismiss, demote,

harass, discriminate against, or cause detriment to a person for making

a protected disclosure.

Civil and criminal immunity --- a whistleblower is not liable for breach

of confidentiality or other legal obligations by making a protected

disclosure.

6. Investigation

All reports will be acknowledged within 5 business days. An impartial investigation will be conducted by a Director not involved in the matter, or by an external investigator if appropriate. The discloser will be kept informed of progress where their identity is known.

7. Victimisation

Any person who victimises, threatens or disadvantages a whistleblower --- or attempts to identify one --- commits a serious disciplinary offence and may commit a criminal offence under the Corporations Act.

C4 Section C — HR & Workplace

Sexual Harassment, Bullying & Discrimination Policy (Staff)

PurposeTo provide a workplace free from sexual harassment,

Scope All staff, Directors, contractors, volunteers and

visitors.

Legal Basis Equal Opportunity Act 2010 (Vic); Sex

Discrimination Act 1984 (Cth); Fair Work Act 2009

(Cth); OHS Act 2004 (Vic).

Review Cycle Annual

1. Policy Statement

Consensus 21 Education Ltd is committed to providing a safe, respectful and inclusive workplace. Sexual harassment, bullying and unlawful discrimination in any form are unacceptable and will not be tolerated.

2. Definitions

### Sexual Harassment

### Unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favours, or other conduct of a sexual nature that a reasonable person would anticipate would cause offence, humiliation or intimidation. This includes physical, verbal, written and electronic conduct.

### Workplace Bullying

### Repeated, unreasonable behaviour directed towards a worker or group of workers that creates a risk to health and safety. A single serious incident may also constitute bullying. Reasonable management action taken in a reasonable manner is not bullying.

### Unlawful Discrimination

### Treating a person less favourably because of a protected attribute, including age, disability, ethnicity, gender, parental status, pregnancy, race, religion, sex, or sexual orientation.

3. Responsibilities

All staff are responsible for:

Treating colleagues with dignity, respect and professionalism.

Reporting conduct that may breach this policy.

Cooperating with any investigation.

The Principal is responsible for:

Modelling appropriate behaviour.

Responding promptly to complaints.

Taking reasonable steps to prevent breaches.

4. Complaint Process

The complainant raises the matter with the Principal (or Board Chair if

the complaint involves the Principal).

The Principal meets with the complainant and, where appropriate,

attempts informal resolution.

Where informal resolution is not appropriate or unsuccessful, a formal

investigation is initiated.

Both parties have the right to be heard and to have a support person

present.

The outcome is communicated to both parties in writing.

A record of the complaint and outcome is retained confidentially.

5. External Complaints

Any person may make a complaint to the Victorian Equal Opportunity and Human Rights Commission (VEOHRC) or the Fair Work Commission, regardless of whether an internal complaint has been made.

6. Consequences

Substantiated breaches of this policy may result in disciplinary action up to and including dismissal. False or malicious complaints also constitute misconduct.

C5 Section C — HR & Workplace

Occupational Health & Safety Policy

PurposeTo provide and maintain a safe and healthy

Scope All staff, students, contractors, volunteers and

visitors.

Legal Basis Occupational Health and Safety Act 2004 (Vic); OHS

Regulations 2017 (Vic); WorkCover legislation.

Review Cycle Annual

1. Policy Statement

Consensus 21 Education Ltd is committed to providing and maintaining a working and learning environment that is safe and without risk to the health of all staff, students and visitors. Health and safety is everyone\'s responsibility.

2. Responsibilities

### The Board

Allocates adequate resources for OHS.

Reviews OHS performance annually.

Ensures the Principal has appropriate OHS training and support.

### The Principal

Implements and monitors this policy.

Ensures hazard identification and risk assessment processes are in

place.

Ensures all staff receive OHS induction and training.

Reports notifiable incidents to WorkSafe Victoria.

### All Staff

Comply with OHS policies and procedures.

Report hazards, near-misses and incidents promptly.

Do not undertake work they believe to be unsafe.

Cooperate with the Company\'s OHS management system.

3. Hazard Identification and Risk Management

The school conducts regular workplace inspections and risk assessments. All identified hazards are recorded in the Hazard Register and addressed in order of priority using the hierarchy of controls: eliminate, substitute, isolate, engineer, administer, PPE.

4. Incident Reporting

All injuries, illnesses and near-miss incidents must be reported to the Principal on the day they occur. Serious incidents (as defined by the OHS Act) must be reported to WorkSafe Victoria immediately. All incidents are investigated to prevent recurrence.

5. Consultation

The school consults with staff on OHS matters through regular staff meetings and, where required, through health and safety representative processes. Staff are encouraged to raise OHS concerns at any time.

6. Emergency Procedures

Emergency evacuation procedures are posted throughout the campus and practised at least once per term. First aid facilities are maintained as per the First Aid Policy (A1). An emergency management plan is maintained and reviewed annually.

All events are recorded in the Emergency Incident Log.

4. Lockdown Procedure

A lockdown is implemented when there is a threat requiring all persons to shelter in place (e.g. armed intruder, external hazardous materials incident).

Chief Warden announces lockdown via PA system or personal communication.

All persons move immediately to the nearest lockdown room and

lock/barricade doors.

Lights are turned off, blinds closed, phones silenced.

Chief Warden contacts 000 and advises of the situation.

No one opens doors until police give the all-clear.

Parents are notified as soon as it is safe to do so.

5. Drills and Training

Evacuation drills are conducted at least once per term.

Lockdown drills are conducted at least once per year.

All staff are trained in emergency procedures on induction and annually.

Drills are reviewed and improvements recorded.

6. Communication

In an emergency, the Communications Officer manages all external communications. Parents are notified via SMS/email as soon as it is safe to do so. Media inquiries are directed to the Board Chair or Principal only.

7. Recovery

Following an emergency, the Principal will arrange psychological first aid and debrief for affected staff and students. A post-incident review will be conducted within 5 business days and improvements implemented.

1. Mandatory Reporting Obligations

Under the Children, Youth and Families Act 2005 (Vic), registered teachers and other specified persons are mandatory reporters. A mandatory reporter must make a report to Child Protection (DFFH) if they form a reasonable belief that a child has suffered, or is likely to suffer, significant harm as a result of physical or sexual abuse, and the child\'s parents have not protected or are unlikely to protect the child from that harm.

2. What is a \'Reasonable Belief\'?

A reasonable belief exists when a person, aware of the circumstances, would believe that an act or omission has caused or is likely to cause significant harm. You do not need proof --- if you are uncertain, you should still report. It is not your job to investigate; it is your job to report.

3. Additional Obligations

### Failure to Disclose (s.327 Crimes Act)

### Any adult who holds information that leads them to form a reasonable belief that a sexual offence has been committed against a child under 16 by another adult must disclose that information to police. Failure to do so without reasonable excuse is a criminal offence.

### Failure to Protect (s.49O Crimes Act)

### A person in a position of authority in an organisation who knew of a substantial risk that another person would commit a sexual offence against a child under 16 in the organisation\'s care, and had the power to reduce or remove that risk but negligently failed to do so, commits a criminal offence.

### Grooming (s.49M Crimes Act)

### It is a criminal offence for a person (aged 18+) to communicate with a child or their parents/carers with the intention of facilitating child sexual abuse.

4. How to Make a Report

Where a child is in immediate danger, call 000.

Contact the Child Protection intake line: 13 12 78 (24 hours).

Notify the Principal of any report made (unless the Principal is the

subject of the concern, in which case notify the Board Chair directly).

Document the concerns, observations and actions taken.

Mandatory reporters who make a report in good faith are protected from

civil and criminal liability even if the report turns out to be

unsubstantiated.

5. Internal Reporting

Duty of Care Policy

PurposeTo ensure all staff understand and fulfil their

Scope All staff and volunteers with responsibility for

students.

Legal Basis Common law duty of care; Education and Training

Reform Act 2006 (Vic); OHS Act 2004 (Vic).

Review Cycle Annual

1. What is Duty of Care?

A duty of care is a legal and ethical obligation to take reasonable steps to avoid causing foreseeable harm. Schools and their staff have a non-delegable duty of care to students while they are at school or engaged in school activities. This duty requires staff to act as a \'reasonable and prudent parent\' would in the same circumstances.

2. Scope of Duty

The school\'s duty of care applies:

During all school hours on the school site.

During all excursions, camps, sport and other school-organised

activities.

Where the school has assumed control of students (e.g. at a transport

pick-up point).

In some circumstances, to conduct off-campus that impacts student

wellbeing at school.

3. Responsibilities of Staff

Be present and attentive during supervision times.

Anticipate risks and act to prevent foreseeable harm.

Follow school policies on supervision, first aid, behaviour management

and child safety.

Not leave students unsupervised.

Report concerns about student wellbeing promptly.

Maintain appropriate professional boundaries with students at all times.

4. Limits of Duty

The duty of care does not make staff insurers of student safety against all risks. The standard is one of reasonableness. Minor risks that are inherent in normal childhood activity do not require elimination --- they require management through reasonable precautions.

5. Breach of Duty

A breach of duty occurs when a staff member fails to take the steps a reasonable person in their position would have taken. Breaches may result in:

Civil liability for the school and/or individual staff member.

Disciplinary action by the school.

Referral to the VIT or other regulatory body.

6. Professional Boundaries

Staff must maintain professional boundaries with students at all times, including:

Not engaging in one-on-one unsupervised contact with students.

Not communicating with students via personal social media or messaging

applications.

G4 Section G — Child Safety

Working with Children Policy

PurposeTo ensure all persons working with children at the
ScopeAll staff, Directors, contractors and volunteers
Legal BasisWorking with Children Act 2005 (Vic); VRQA Minimum

Standards; MO 1359.

Review Cycle Annual

1. Policy Statement

Consensus 21 Education Ltd requires all persons working with children at Peninsula 21 to hold a current Working with Children Check (WWCC) issued by the Department of Justice and Community Safety (Victoria). The WWCC is a mandatory child safety requirement and compliance is non-negotiable.

2. Who Needs a WWCC?

All teachers and other employees who have direct contact with students.

All volunteers who assist with school activities involving students.

All contractors and service providers who work on the school site when

students are present.

All Directors of the Company (as \'officers\' of an organisation

providing services to children).

Note: A Registered Teaching Certificate from the Victorian Institute of

Teaching (VIT) satisfies the WWCC requirement for registered teachers.

3. Verification and Monitoring

The Principal verifies WWCC status for all persons before they commence

work or volunteering.

WWCC status is recorded in the WWCC Register.

The Register is reviewed termly to identify expiring checks.

Any person whose WWCC expires or is revoked must immediately cease

working with children until a valid check is obtained.

4. Negative Notices

A person who has been issued a negative notice by the Department of Justice must not be engaged in any child-related work. If the school becomes aware that a person has received a negative notice, they must be immediately stood down from work involving children.

5. Responsibility

The Principal is responsible for maintaining the WWCC Register and

monitoring compliance.

All persons required to hold a WWCC are responsible for ensuring their

check remains current.

H1 Section H — Digital Technologies & Reporting

Section H --- Digital Technologies & Reporting

PurposeTo enable safe, responsible and effective use of
ScopeAll students, staff and volunteers.
Legal BasisPrivacy and Data Protection Act 2014 (Vic); Privacy

Review Cycle Annual

1. Policy Statement

Consensus 21 Education Ltd embraces digital technology as a powerful tool for learning, creativity and collaboration. All members of the school community are expected to use digital technology safely, ethically and in a manner consistent with the school\'s values and this policy.

2. Acceptable Use --- Students

Students may use school devices and networks for learning purposes. Students must:

Use devices only for school-related purposes during class time.

Treat devices with care and respect.

Not access, create or share content that is harmful, offensive,

discriminatory, or illegal.

Not access social media during school hours unless specifically directed

by a teacher for educational purposes.

Not share personal information online without teacher approval.

Report any concerning online content or contact to a staff member

immediately.

3. Acceptable Use --- Staff

Staff may use school devices and systems for professional purposes. Staff must:

Not communicate with students via personal social media or messaging

applications.

Maintain student data in accordance with the Privacy Policy (C2).

Use school email systems for official school communications.

Report any cybersecurity incidents or data breaches to the Principal

immediately.

4. Internet Safety

The school maintains filtered internet access for students.

Digital literacy and online safety is embedded in the curriculum at

every year level.

Students are taught to recognise and respond to cyberbullying,

inappropriate contact, and online risks.

5. Personal Devices --- Students

Students may bring personal devices (phones, tablets) to school subject to the following:

Phones must be stored in a designated location during class time.

Personal devices may be used at recess and lunch at the student\'s risk.

The school accepts no liability for loss, damage, or theft of personal

devices.

Devices found to have been used to bully, harass or harm others will be

confiscated and parents notified.

6. Social Media

The school\'s social media accounts are managed by the Principal or a designated staff member. Staff personal social media use must not:

Identify students or share images without parental consent.

Comment negatively on the school, colleagues, students or families.

Share confidential information about the school or its community.

7. Breaches

Breaches of this policy by students are addressed through the Behaviour Management Policy. Breaches by staff are a disciplinary matter and may be referred to the VIT.
